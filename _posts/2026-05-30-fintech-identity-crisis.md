A FinTech that processes payments, holds customer data, and builds software for bank partners is simultaneously a technology company, a financial services company, and a regulated entity. Insurance underwriting has a category for each of those. It does not have a category for the combination.

The result is an insurance program assembled from pieces that were never designed to work together. Five to eight policies, purchased from different carriers, placed by different brokers, at different times, to satisfy different stakeholders. Each policy written as if the company only does one thing. The program looks complete on a certificate of insurance. It falls apart under a claim.

## Tech Company or Financial Institution? Pick One

Insurance underwriters classify companies into risk categories. Banks go in one bucket. Software companies go in another. A FinTech that lends money through a proprietary algorithm, processes payments through a bank partner's infrastructure, and stores customer financial data in its own cloud environment does not fit in either bucket.

What happens in practice: the FinTech buys a Technology Errors & Omissions policy from one carrier and a Financial Institution Professional Liability policy from another. The Tech E&O covers software failures. The Financial E&O covers errors in financial services delivery. When a claim spans both, and most real incidents do, the carriers point at each other.

A platform outage that causes incorrect transaction processing and triggers a regulatory investigation involves technology failure, financial harm, and regulatory exposure simultaneously. The Tech E&O carrier says it is a financial services matter. The Financial E&O carrier says it is a technology failure. This is not a hypothetical. It is how coverage disputes work when two policies from different carriers cover adjacent but non-overlapping risks.

I call this **The Identity Crisis**. It is the defining structural problem in FinTech insurance.

A traditional technology company does not hold customer funds. A traditional financial institution does not build software. A FinTech does both. Insurance underwriting still treats these as separate worlds.

## Five Policies, Four Stakeholders, Zero Coordination

The identity crisis is the cause. The coordination failure is the consequence.

A Series B FinTech operating in multiple states typically carries five to eight policies. Each one was purchased to satisfy a different stakeholder at a different time.

Nobody coordinates these requirements into a coherent program. The broker who placed the Tech E&O does not know what the Financial E&O excludes. The broker who placed the D&O does not know what the cyber policy defines as a "wrongful act." The result: overlapping coverage in some areas, gaps in others, and contradictory definitions across policies that surface at claim time.

I have seen FinTechs spend over $300,000 on insurance before generating revenue, solely to satisfy a single bank partnership requirement. That spend bought certificates. It did not buy coordinated protection.

## The Product Exists. The Distribution Does Not.

At least one specialist underwriter built a combined FinTech policy form in 2018 that blends technology E&O, financial services E&O, D&O, crime, cyber, and employment practices liability into a single policy. The product solves the identity crisis by design. One carrier, one form, one set of definitions. No finger-pointing between policies because there is only one policy.

A second program, built as a partnership between a global broker and a carrier, offers a similar blended approach. It is only available through that broker's own distribution.

These products exist. Most FinTechs have never seen them.

The blended form is available through specialist channels that most FinTech brokers do not access. A generalist broker, or even a startup-focused platform optimized for speed of quoting, typically places standard technology policies or standard financial institution policies. They do not access the specialty markets where the combined form lives. The FinTech ends up with two mismatched policies not because a better product does not exist, but because nobody told them it did.

The insurance product that solves the FinTech identity crisis was built eight years ago. The advisory and distribution layer that connects FinTechs to that product still does not exist for most companies in the segment.

## Where the Program Breaks

Beyond the identity crisis itself, two patterns show up repeatedly in how FinTech insurance programs are structured.

### The Paper Shield

A bank partner contract requires $5 million in Cyber and Crime coverage. The FinTech has a $5 million cyber policy. It does not have a standalone crime policy.

This matters because cyber insurance and crime insurance cover different things. Cyber covers data breaches: notification costs, forensic investigation, regulatory defense, business interruption from system outages. Crime covers stolen money: social engineering fraud, funds transfer fraud, employee theft.

A FinTech that processes payments, holds float, or influences the flow of funds needs both. Many carry only cyber. The bank partner contract technically requires crime coverage, but the bank checks the certificate, sees $5 million in coverage, and reads "cyber" as "everything digital." After the Synapse collapse in 2024, where $265 million in customer funds were frozen and bank partners ratcheted up insurance requirements across the industry, that level of scrutiny is changing.

### The Fundraising Freeze

D&O insurance is typically the first policy a FinTech buys, purchased at Series A to satisfy investor term sheet requirements. The purchase happens under time pressure. The founder accepts whatever the broker recommends to close the round.

Two years later, the company has tripled in size, launched new products, entered new states, added regulated activities. The D&O policy language has not changed since inception. The limits that were adequate for a 15-person pre-revenue startup may not be adequate for a 60-person company processing $50 million in annual transactions across 12 states. Nobody triggered a review because there was no trigger event. The round closed. The policy renewed on autopilot.

## What You Should Be Able to Answer

If you run a FinTech or manage its insurance program, you should be able to answer these five questions about your own coverage. Not your broker. You.

<div><strong>When was the last time your D&O policy language was reviewed against your current operations?</strong> If the answer is "at Series A," the policy reflects a company that no longer exists.</div>
</div>
<div style="display: flex; gap: 16px;">
<div style="min-width: 32px; height: 32px; background: #00D4AA; border-radius: 50%; display: flex; align-items: center; justify-content: center; font-weight: 700; color: #0A2540; font-size: 16px;">5</div>
<div><strong>Has anyone ever read all of your policies together?</strong> Not the certificates. Not the declarations pages. The actual policy forms, tested against each other for overlaps, gaps, and contradictions.</div>

If nobody in your company can answer question five, that is the finding.
