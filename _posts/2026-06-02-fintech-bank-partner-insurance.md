Before a bank partner will let a FinTech touch its infrastructure, it mandates insurance. Tech E&O at specific limits. Cyber liability with defined sub-coverages. Crime or fidelity coverage. Additional Insured status. These requirements are spelled out in the partnership agreement, usually in a section nobody reads after the deal closes.

The requirements are non-negotiable. They are also, in most cases, unverified. The bank checks the certificate of insurance. Nobody checks the policy language behind it.

## The Certificate Is Not the Policy

A certificate of insurance confirms three things: a policy exists, it is currently active, and it carries certain limits. That is all it does.

A bank partner agreement says something more specific. It does not just require "$5 million in cyber liability." It requires coverage for unauthorized access to computer systems, data breach notification and response costs, regulatory fines and penalties, business interruption from system outages, and social engineering fraud. Whether the policy behind the certificate actually delivers all of those things is a question the certificate does not answer.

The certificate says $5M Cyber Liability. The partnership agreement lists twelve specific coverage requirements across four policy types. The gap between those two documents is where claims get denied.

## The Paper Shield

I call this gap **The Paper Shield**: a FinTech's insurance program satisfies the bank partner's requirements on paper but not in the policy language.

The most common version: the bank partner contract requires both Cyber and Crime coverage. The FinTech has a cyber policy at $5 million. It does not have a standalone crime policy.

This matters because cyber insurance and crime insurance cover different losses.

A FinTech with a $5 million cyber policy and no crime coverage is technically non-compliant with a bank partner agreement that specifies both. In practice, nobody catches this because the certificate does not break coverage into those categories. It lists a policy type and a limit. The bank's compliance team checks the box and moves on.

That level of scrutiny is changing.

## What Changed After Synapse

In 2024, Synapse Financial Technologies collapsed. Approximately $265 million in customer funds were frozen across multiple bank partners. The Federal Reserve issued a cease-and-desist order to Evolve Bank for "unsafe and unsound banking practices" in managing its FinTech partnerships. The FDIC proposed new rules requiring banks to maintain detailed ledgers of customer funds held through FinTech intermediaries.

The practical effect on insurance: bank partners ratcheted up requirements. Partnership agreements that previously required $1 million in cyber coverage now require $3 million to $5 million across multiple coverage types, with specific sub-coverage mandates. FinTechs that were grandfathered under older, lighter requirements are being asked to re-certify.

For the first time, some bank compliance teams are looking past the certificate and asking to see the actual policy forms. They want to verify that the coverage types specified in the partnership agreement exist as defined coverages in the policy, not just as line items on a certificate.

## Additional Insured Does Not Mean What You Think

Bank partner contracts often require the FinTech to name the bank as an Additional Insured on its Tech E&O and cyber policies. The assumption is that this gives the bank its own coverage under the FinTech's policy if a claim arises from the partnership.

Three problems with that assumption.

First, many Tech E&O policies do not offer Additional Insured endorsements at all. The policy form was not designed for it. If the endorsement is not available, the FinTech cannot comply with the contract requirement, and most FinTechs do not know this until the bank asks for proof.

Second, when the endorsement is available, it may not extend to the types of claims the bank expects it to cover. An Additional Insured endorsement on a Tech E&O policy typically covers the bank for claims arising from the FinTech's professional services. It does not necessarily cover the bank for regulatory investigations, data breach liabilities, or its own defense costs in a lawsuit related to the FinTech's operations.

Third, the bank partner's own insurance counsel may not have tested whether the Additional Insured endorsement works under the specific policy form the FinTech carries. The contract says "name us as Additional Insured." The FinTech's broker adds the endorsement. Whether the endorsement actually delivers the protection the bank expects is a question nobody asks until a claim forces the answer.

## What You Should Be Able to Answer

These are not questions for your broker. These are questions you should be able to answer about your own insurance program before your bank partner asks them.

<div><strong>Pull the insurance requirements section from your bank partner agreement.</strong> List every coverage type, every minimum limit, and every condition (Additional Insured, waiver of subrogation, notice of cancellation).</div>
</div>

<div><strong>Match each requirement to the actual policy form, not the certificate.</strong> If the contract requires "Crime" coverage and you only have a cyber policy, check whether your cyber policy includes a social engineering or funds transfer fraud insuring agreement. If it does not, you have a gap.</div>
</div>
<div style="display: flex; gap: 16px; margin-bottom: 20px;">
<div style="min-width: 32px; height: 32px; background: #00D4AA; border-radius: 50%; display: flex; align-items: center; justify-content: center; font-weight: 700; color: #0A2540; font-size: 16px;">3</div>
<div><strong>If Additional Insured status is required, confirm the endorsement exists on your policy and review what it covers.</strong> An endorsement that covers the bank for "claims arising from the insured's professional services" may not cover the bank for regulatory investigations or its own breach liabilities.</div>
</div>
<div style="display: flex; gap: 16px; margin-bottom: 20px;">
<div style="min-width: 32px; height: 32px; background: #00D4AA; border-radius: 50%; display: flex; align-items: center; justify-content: center; font-weight: 700; color: #0A2540; font-size: 16px;">4</div>
<div><strong>Check whether your partnership agreement has been updated since Synapse.</strong> If your coverage was structured to meet pre-2024 requirements and the agreement has been amended, the gap may have widened without any change in your policy.</div>
</div>
<div style="display: flex; gap: 16px;">
<div style="min-width: 32px; height: 32px; background: #00D4AA; border-radius: 50%; display: flex; align-items: center; justify-content: center; font-weight: 700; color: #0A2540; font-size: 16px;">5</div>
<div><strong>Ask whether anyone has read the policy forms and the partnership agreement side by side.</strong> Not the certificate against the agreement. The actual policy language against the actual contract language. If the answer is no, that is the gap.</div>
</div>
</div>

If nobody in your company can answer question five, your bank partner will eventually ask it for you. Better to know the answer first.
